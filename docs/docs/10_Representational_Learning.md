
# 10 Representational Learning

## 10.1 Introduction

- representational learning is approached through **generative models** that attempt to explain sensory data (like images or neural spikes) by identifying their underlying "causes". It is a cycle between two models: 
    - one that generates "fake" data from causes 
    - another that recognizes the causes of "real" data

- The Generative Model: From Causes to Data
    - The goal of a generative model is to simulate the statistical structure of the world. We start with an **underlying cause** (denoted as $v$) and want to produce an **input** (denoted as $u$)

- The Prior Distribution: $ \gamma_v := P[v; G] $
- The Generative Distribution: $ p[u|v; G] $
- The Marginal Distribution: $ p[u; G] = \sum_{v} p[u|v; G] P[v; G] $
- The Posterior Recognition Distribution (**Bayes' Theorem**)

$$ P[v|u; G] = \frac{p[u|v; G]P[v; G]}{p[u; G]}$$

- The Learning Process: Expectation Maximization (EM)
    - Learning involves adjusting the parameters $G$ so that the synthetic data generated by the model matches the real-world data $p[u]$
    - This is done by minimizing the **Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence**:

    $$ D_{KL}(p[u], p[u; G]) = \int du \, p[u] \ln \frac{p[u]}{p[u; G]} $$

    - Since we don't know the "true" causes of the real data, we use the **EM algorithm** to iteratively guess them

- Step 1: The E (Expectation) Phase
    - We use our *current* parameters to calculate  $P[v|u; G]$ for every data point. This is our "best guess" at the cause

- Step 2: The M-Phase (The Optimization Step)
    - Once we have these $P[v|u; G]$, we have a "complete" dataset (data + guessed causes). 
    - The equations we see in our code (the weighted averages) are actually the closed-form solutions for maximizing the likelihood for a Gaussian distribution.
    - By setting the new mean to the weighted average of the points, we are mathematically moving the center of that Gaussian to the location that makes those points most likely to have occurred.
    - If the model's "guess" (marginal distribution) doesn't match the real data, the KL divergence is high. Each iteration of EM is guaranteed to either increase the likelihood or keep it the same, effectively "climbing the hill" toward the optimal parameters.

$$ \gamma_{v} = \langle P[v|u;G] \rangle \\[5pt]
\mu_v = \frac{\langle P[v|u;G] \; u \rangle}{\gamma_v} $$





## 10.2 Density Estimation

- Step 1: The Goal (Matching Reality)
    - The ultimate objective of any learning system is to make its internal model of the world, $p[u;G]$, identical to the actual input distribution of the real world, $p[u]$ 
    - **The Measurement**: We use **Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence** to measure the "distance" or discrepancy between these two distributions

    $$ D_{KL}(p[u], p[u;G]) = 
    \underbrace{\int du \ p[u] \ln p[u]}_{\text{Entropy of Reality (Constant } K)} 
    - \underbrace{\int du \ p[u] \ln p[u;G]}_{\text{Average Log Likelihood } L(G)} $$ 

    - **The Logic**: Since the entropy of reality ($K$) is constant and cannot be changed, the only way to minimize the discrepancy is to **maximize the Average Log Likelihood $L(G)$**

- Step 2: The "Hidden Cause" Wall
    - In a causal model, the input $u$ (like an image) is generated by a hidden cause $v$ (like the concept of a "tree"). To calculate the probability of the input $p[u;G]$, you must sum across every possible cause:

    $$ p[u;G] = \sum_{v} p[u, v; G] $$

    - **The Problem**: In real life, the number of possible causes $v$ is so massive that this sum is impossible to compute directly
    - **The Need**: We need a way to maximize $L(G)$ without actually calculating it.

- Step 3: The Workaround (Free Energy $\mathcal{F}$)
    - We introduce an **Approximate Recognition Distribution**, $Q[v;u]$, which is simply our "best guess" for the cause of a specific input. We then define a new function called **Free Energy ($\mathcal{F}$)**. 
    - To solve the "Impossible Sum" problem, $\mathcal{F}$ is given **two different mathematical identities**:
    - Identity A: The "Justification" (Why it works)
        
        $$\mathcal{F}(Q, G) = L(G) - \langle D_{KL}(Q[v;u], P[v|u;G]) \rangle$$
        
        - **Reasoning**: Because $D_{KL}$ is never negative, $\mathcal{F}$ is a **lower bound** for $L(G)$. If we push $\mathcal{F}$ up, $L(G)$ must go up too.
        - **Confusion Fix**: We don't use this version for calculations; we use it only to prove that climbing $\mathcal{F}$ is equivalent to climbing $L(G)$.

    - Identity B: The "Calculation" (How we do it)
        
        $$ \mathcal{F}(Q, G) = \langle \sum_{v} Q[v;u] \ln \frac{p[v, u; G]}{Q[v;u]} \rangle$$ 
        
        - **Reasoning**: This version uses the **Joint Distribution** $p[v, u; G]$
        - **Justification**: The joint distribution describes how a specific cause leads to a specific input. This is the "generative rule" of our model, which is usually very easy to calculate. **The "Impossible Sum" from Step 2 is gone.**

- Step 4: The EM Algorithm (The "Two-Step Dance")
    - We maximize $\mathcal{F}$ by alternating between two phases, effectively climbing the likelihood mountain.
    - Phase 1: The E Phase (Perception)
        - **Action**: Hold the model ($G$) fixed and update our guesses ($Q$)
        - **Goal**: Make our guesses $Q$ as close as possible to the true recognition $P$
        - **Effect**: This reduces the $D_{KL}$ gap in **Identity A** to zero, making $\mathcal{F}$ exactly equal to $L(G)$
    - Phase 2: The M Phase (Learning)
        - **Action**: Hold our guesses ($Q$) fixed and update the model knobs ($G$)
        - **Goal**: Maximize $\mathcal{F}$ using the easy-to-calculate **Identity B**
        - **Effect**: Even though $Q$ and $P$ drift apart slightly as the model changes, $\mathcal{F}$ increases, which forces the actual likelihood $L(G)$ to increase along with it

- Step 5: The Biological Interpretation
    - In simple (invertible) models, the E phase is just a math problem we solve. But in complex biological (noninvertible) models, we can't be perfect
    - **Variational Methods**: The brain uses a simplified "shape" for $Q$ (like a set of parameters $W$) and finds the best parameters to get as close as possible to the truth
    - **The Result**: The brain doesn't need to see the "Likelihood" directly. By minimizing its internal "surprise" or discrepancy (Free Energy), it is mathematically guaranteed to learn a better model of the world over time
